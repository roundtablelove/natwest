# Legal Strategy — Litigant in Person, No Solicitor

    Relates: IR-20210921-NATWEST, ENQ-20210921-001
   Strategy: Private prosecution → press → CPS takeover → enquiry forced
     Status: PLANNING

All filings below can be made without a solicitor. Every court and tribunal
listed is designed to accept litigants in person. The system does not want you
to know this.

## 1. Overview — The Sequence

The strategy has a specific order. Each step creates pressure for the next. Do
not scatter. Execute in sequence.

```
Phase 1: EVIDENCE       Obtain the 999 recording. Compare with video.
Phase 2: CRIMINAL       Lay private prosecution. Summons issued.
Phase 3: PRESS          Go public. The summons is the story.
Phase 4: CPS TAKEOVER   CPS takes over under s.6(2). Now they own it.
Phase 5: REGULATORY     FCA, FOS, ICO complaints — concurrent pressure.
Phase 6: CIVIL          County Court claim for damages.
Phase 7: ENQUIRY        FOI data reveals pattern. Press has the story.
                         CPS already involved. Pattern forces enquiry.
```

## 3. Phase 2 — Private Prosecution

### 3.1. The Right

Any person may bring a private prosecution in England and Wales. This is a
constitutional right preserved by **s.6(1) Prosecution of Offences Act 1985**.
You do not need a solicitor. You do not need the CPS. You do not need police
permission.

### 3.2. The Offence

**Wasting police time — Criminal Law Act 1967, s.5(2):**

> Where a person causes any wasteful employment of the police by knowingly
> making to any person a false report [...] he shall be liable on summary
> conviction to imprisonment for not more than six months [...]

Elements to prove:

1. A report was made to police (the 999 call — Met SAR will confirm).
2. The report was false — i.e. it contained claims about the complainant's
   behaviour that did not occur (video evidence vs. 999 recording).
3. The report caused wasteful employment of police — officers attended the
   complainant's home for a non-crime (dispatch records will confirm).
4. The person making the report knew it was false or was reckless as to whether
   it was false.

If the 999 recording shows significant fabrication or embellishment, consider
the more serious charge:

**Perverting the course of justice — Common Law offence:**

- Indictable only (Crown Court).
- Maximum sentence: life imprisonment.
- Elements: an act or series of acts that has or have a tendency to pervert the
  course of public justice.
- Making a false report to police that results in deployment to an innocent
  person's home is capable of satisfying this test.

### 3.3. How to File

1. **Attend the local Magistrates' Court** (Camberwell Green Magistrates' Court
   or Westminster Magistrates' Court).
2. **Lay an information** — this is a formal written statement setting out:
   - The defendant (the person who made the 999 call — name to be obtained from
     NatWest SAR and Met SAR).
   - The offence (s.5(2) Criminal Law Act 1967).
   - The facts — date, time, location, what happened, what the 999 call said vs.
     what the video shows.
3. **The magistrate decides whether to issue a summons.** The test is whether
   the information discloses a prima facie case. You have video. You will have
   the 999 recording. If there is a material discrepancy, the case is prima
   facie made out.
4. **Summons issued.** The defendant must appear before the court. This is a
   criminal summons. It is not optional.

### 3.4. Cost

Laying an information is free. There is no court fee for a private prosecution
at the magistrates' court. If the case proceeds and you are successful, you can
apply for costs from central funds under **s.17 Prosecution of Offences Act
1985**.

### 3.5. The Defendant

The defendant is the individual who made or authorised the 999 call. Not
"NatWest." A person. The SARs will identify who made the call. The manager is
the likely defendant, but the caller may have been another member of staff
acting on the manager's instruction — in which case both may be liable (the
caller as principal, the manager as accessory or joint principal).

## 4. Phase 3 — Press

### 4.1. Timing

Go to press **after** the summons is issued. Not before. The summons is the
story. Without it, the story is "man angry at bank" — not news. With the
summons, the story is "bank customer privately prosecutes NatWest manager for
making a false 999 call after refusing him his own money." That is news.

### 4.2. The Package

Provide to journalists:

- This incident report ([README.md](README.md)).
- The two videos.
- The transcript.
- The stills.
- The summons (once issued).
- The enquiry document ([ENQUIRY.md](ENQUIRY.md)) — the pattern question.
- Mr C's case (once details confirmed) — evidence this is not isolated.

### 4.3. Target Outlets

| Outlet | Why |
| :--- | :--- |
| Local press (Brixton Blog, South London Press) | Community interest. Brixton branch, Brixton residents. |
| The Guardian | Banking, policing, race, institutional accountability. |
| The Bureau of Investigative Journalism | Data-driven investigation. FOI pattern data is their territory. |
| Private Eye | Corporate accountability, police accountability. |
| BBC London / ITV London | Video is broadcast-ready. Man recorded his own refusal of service. |
| Channel 4 News | Long-form. Pattern enquiry angle. |

### 4.4. The Frame

The story is not "angry man vs. bank." The story is:

> A lifelong NatWest customer presented valid ID at his home branch to withdraw
> his own money. The manager refused. The customer expressed anger. The manager
> called 999 after the customer had already left and gone home. Police arrived
> within five minutes. The customer has video of the entire interaction showing
> he made no threat. He is now privately prosecuting the manager for wasting
> police time — and asking how many other people this has happened to.

The enquiry question is the force multiplier. One incident is a complaint.
A pattern is a scandal.

## 5. Phase 4 — CPS Takeover

### 5.1. The Mechanism

Under **s.6(2) Prosecution of Offences Act 1985**, the Director of Public
Prosecutions (CPS) has the right to take over any private prosecution at any
stage. Once taken over, the CPS can either:

- **Continue** the prosecution, or
- **Discontinue** it.

### 5.2. Why This Works For You Either Way

**If the CPS continues:** The state is now prosecuting a NatWest manager for
making a false 999 call. You have transferred the burden. The CPS has the
resources, the disclosure powers, and the institutional weight. You step back.
The press story escalates: "CPS takes over private prosecution of NatWest
manager."

**If the CPS discontinues:** They must give reasons. The discontinuation is
itself a story. "CPS drops prosecution of NatWest manager who called 999 on
customer who had already gone home — despite video evidence." The press runs
that. Public pressure mounts. The FOI data (Phase 7) showing a pattern lands
on top.

**If the CPS does nothing:** The private prosecution continues. You present your
case. You have video. You have (by then) the 999 recording. The magistrate
hears both. The press covers the trial.

### 5.3. Forcing the CPS's Hand

The CPS does not want to take over nuisance cases. Three things force their
hand:

1. **Press coverage** — once the story is public, doing nothing is a position.
2. **Pattern evidence** — if the FOI data shows NatWest Brixton Road has a
   history of false reports, the CPS faces "why did you ignore a pattern?"
3. **A second complainant** — Mr C. If two people have been falsely reported by
   the same branch, the CPS cannot dismiss it as a grudge prosecution.

## 6. Phase 5 — Regulatory Complaints (Concurrent)

These run in parallel with Phases 2–4. Each creates an independent pressure
track.

### 6.1. Financial Ombudsman Service (FOS)

- **When:** 8 weeks after the [NatWest complaint](complaint-natwest.eml), or
  upon receipt of NatWest's final response, whichever is earlier.
- **How:** Online form at financial-ombudsman.org.uk. No solicitor needed.
- **What:** Refusal of service with valid ID. False 999 report.  Data
  protection breach (disclosure to police without lawful basis).
- **Outcome:** FOS can order compensation and require NatWest to change
  procedures.

### 6.2. FCA Direct

- **How:** Report via FCA online reporting form. FCA does not handle individual
  complaints but uses reports to build supervisory cases.
- **What:** Breach of FCA Principle 6 (Treating Customers Fairly), FCA
  Principle 3 (Management and Control). Reference the pattern enquiry — this
  is not one incident, it may be systemic.
- **Outcome:** FCA may open a supervisory review of NatWest's branch-level
  security escalation procedures.

### 6.3. Information Commissioner's Office (ICO)

- **How:** Online complaint at ico.org.uk. No solicitor needed.
- **What:** NatWest disclosed personal data to police via 999 call without
  lawful basis. No crime committed. No ongoing threat. The disclosure was not
  necessary for any legitimate purpose under UK GDPR Article 6.
- **Also:** If NatWest or the Met breach the SAR deadline (one month), file a
  separate ICO complaint for each.

### 6.4. Independent Office for Police Conduct (IOPC)

- **How:** Complaint first to Met Professional Standards. If unsatisfied,
  escalate to IOPC.
- **What:** Disproportionate priority dispatch. P1 response to a non-crime,
  non-present subject. Possible institutional bias in dispatch grading for
  corporate callers vs. individual victims of violent crime.

## 7. Phase 6 — Civil Claim

### 7.1. County Court

- **How:** File online at moneyclaims.service.gov.uk (for claims up to
  £100,000) or in person at the County Court.
- **No solicitor needed.** Litigant in person. The small claims track (up to
  £10,000) is specifically designed for unrepresented parties.
- **Court fee:** Sliding scale based on claim value. Fee can be waived or
  reduced on grounds of financial hardship (Form EX160).

### 7.2. Causes of Action

| Claim | Basis |
| :--- | :--- |
| Breach of contract | NatWest refused to provide banking services under the account contract. |
| Negligence | NatWest owed a duty of care and breached it by making a false 999 report. |
| Breach of statutory duty | FCA Principles (via FSMA 2000). |
| Data protection breach | UK GDPR Art.82 — right to compensation for material or non-material damage caused by unlawful processing. The 999 call disclosed personal data without lawful basis. |
| Equality Act 2010, s.29 | If the pattern enquiry reveals discriminatory treatment — refusal of service and police referral based on race or perceived social class. Service provider discrimination. |

### 7.3. Damages

| Head | Basis |
| :--- | :--- |
| Distress | Police attendance at home. False report. Humiliation. |
| Loss of banking relationship | Forced to move to another bank after being a lifelong customer. |
| Data protection compensation | UK GDPR Art.82. |
| Aggravated damages | If the 999 call is shown to contain fabricated claims. |
| Net of £4,000 overdraft | Acknowledged — damages to be reduced by the overdraft taken. Interest will NOT be paid. |

### 7.4. Against Whom

- **NatWest Group PLC** — vicariously liable for the acts of its employees
  acting in the course of their employment.
- **The individual manager** — can be named as a co-defendant. Personally
  liable for the false report.

## 8. Phase 7 — Force the Enquiry

### 8.1. The FOI Requests

File under **Freedom of Information Act 2000, s.1:**

**To the Metropolitan Police:**

> All records of 999 calls originating from NatWest, Brixton Road, London
> SW9 for the period 2011–2021, including: number of calls, CAD records,
> dispatch grading, dispatch outcomes (arrest / no further action / no crime),
> charge outcomes, and demographic data of subjects where held.

**To NatWest** (via FCA or parliamentary question — NatWest is not a public
authority under FOI, but the FCA can compel disclosure under its supervisory
powers, and an MP can ask a parliamentary question of the Treasury):

> How many times has the NatWest Brixton Road branch called 999 in relation
> to a customer in the last 10 years? How many of those calls resulted in
> arrest? How many resulted in charge? How many resulted in conviction?

### 8.2. Pattern + Press + Prosecution = Enquiry

No single filing forces an enquiry. The combination does.

```
PRIVATE PROSECUTION  → The case is before a court.
PRESS COVERAGE       → The public is watching.
FOI DATA             → The pattern is on record.
SECOND COMPLAINANT   → It's not isolated.
REGULATORY COMPLAINTS → FCA, FOS, ICO, IOPC all have open files.
CIVIL CLAIM          → NatWest is defending in County Court.

RESULT: Too many open fronts. NatWest cannot settle quietly because
        the criminal prosecution is public. The CPS cannot ignore it
        because the press is covering it. The FCA cannot ignore it
        because the pattern data exists. The Met cannot ignore it
        because the IOPC complaint is open.

        Someone commissions a review. That review is the enquiry.
```

### 8.3. If Nothing Works — The Nuclear Option

**MP referral.** Write to your MP. Provide the full package. Request a
parliamentary question. An MP can:

- Ask a written or oral question in the House about NatWest's use of 999 to
  manage customer complaints.
- Request that the Treasury Select Committee examine NatWest's branch-level
  security escalation procedures.
- Refer the matter to the All-Party Parliamentary Group on Banking.

This is Brixton. The MP is likely sympathetic. The constituency is
disproportionately affected by exactly this kind of institutional behaviour.
