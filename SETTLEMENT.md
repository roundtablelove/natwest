## 1. The Play

You don't go in asking for a number. You go in showing them the machine — every
filing drafted, every article written, every social media post queued, the
private prosecution ready to lay, the FOI request ready to send, the press kit
ready to distribute, the pattern enquiry ready to publish.

Then you say: **"Or we can talk."**

The package is the negotiation. The number is what they calculate it will cost
them if you press GO.

## 3. What NatWest Is Really Paying For

The numbers above are the legal damages. But NatWest is not settling to avoid a
£70k County Court judgment. NatWest is settling to avoid:

| Threat | Cost to NatWest |
| :--- | :--- |
| Private prosecution of a named manager | Legal fees, HR crisis, staff morale, union issues. Manager's personal legal costs (NatWest likely indemnifies). Board attention. |
| Press coverage | "NatWest called 999 on a Black customer for wanting his own money" — front page of Guardian, lead on Channel 4 News. Reputational damage in the hundreds of thousands. |
| Social media campaign | Sustained, daily, tagged. Every post is a cost to the social media response team. Viral potential of the video is high — a man filmed being refused his own money at his own bank, manager fleeing. |
| Pattern enquiry going public | "How many other people?" is the question NatWest cannot afford to have asked in public. If the FOI data shows a pattern, this is a scandal, not an incident. |
| FCA supervisory attention | An FCA review of branch-level security escalation procedures. Compliance cost. Potential enforcement action. |
| FOS complaint + precedent | FOS can order compensation and require procedure changes. Published decision becomes precedent for other complainants. |
| Equality Act claim | If the pattern data shows demographic skew — this becomes an institutional racism case. EHRC referral. The cost is existential for the brand. |
| Second complainant (Mr C) | Proves it's not a grudge. Proves it's a pattern. Doubles the press story. |

**NatWest's calculus:** The legal damages are £60k–£238k. The reputational
damage from press coverage is £500k+. The cost of an FCA review is millions.
The cost of an institutional racism finding is incalculable.

## 4. Settlement Ranges — Realistic

### 4.1. Before Press (Quiet Settlement)

If you show them the package and they settle before anything goes public:

| Scenario | Range | Likelihood |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| You only, basic claim | £25,000–£50,000 | Moderate — if they think you won't follow through. |
| You only, full package shown | £50,000–£100,000 | Good — they are paying for silence on the pattern question. |
| You + Mr C, full package | £100,000–£200,000 | Good — two complainants, pattern evidence, press-ready. |
| You + Mr C, pattern data obtained | £150,000–£300,000 | High — if FOI data confirms the pattern, NatWest will pay to bury it. |

### 4.2. After Press (Public Settlement)

Once the press has the story, the settlement goes up because NatWest is now also
paying to end the news cycle:

| Scenario | Range |
| :--- | :--- |
| Post-press, pre-prosecution | £100,000–£250,000 |
| Post-summons, CPS watching | £150,000–£350,000 |
| Post-FOI pattern data published | £200,000–£500,000+ |

### 4.3. If It Goes to Trial

County Court judgment for the civil claim: £25,000–£75,000 per claimant (judge
awards based on evidence, not leverage).

But the civil judgment is the least of it. The private prosecution, the press
coverage, the regulatory complaints, and the pattern enquiry continue regardless
of the civil outcome. NatWest cannot make this go away by winning or losing in
County Court. The machine has too many moving parts.

## 5. The Approach — How to Show the Package

### 5.1. Letter Before Action

Before filing anything public, send a Letter Before Action to NatWest's legal
department (not the complaints team — legal). This is a standard pre-litigation
step. It:

- Sets out the claim.
- Identifies the legal basis.
- States the remedy sought.
- Gives a deadline (14 days is standard).

But this letter is different. This letter includes:

- The incident report.
- The bug reports.
- The enquiry document.
- The legal strategy.
- The blitz plan.
- The settlement analysis (this document — or a redacted version).
- A USB stick or secure link with both videos.

The letter says, in effect:

> This is what happened. This is the evidence. These are the legal filings we
> have drafted. This is the press kit we have prepared. This is the pattern
> question we intend to ask publicly. This is the social media campaign we have
> planned.
>
> We would prefer to resolve this without executing the above. We invite you to
> make a settlement offer within 14 days.
>
> If no offer is received, or if the offer is inadequate, we will proceed with
> all filings, publications, and the private prosecution simultaneously.

### 5.2. Who Receives It

- **NatWest Group PLC — General Counsel.** Not complaints. Not customer
  service. Legal.
- **CC: NatWest CEO office.** So the CEO's team knows legal has it.
- **CC: The branch manager (by name, if known from SAR).** So they know
  personally.

### 5.3. The 14-Day Window

During the 14 days:

- Do NOT publish anything.
- Do NOT file anything new.
- Do NOT post on social media.
- Let the silence do the work. They are reading the package. They are
  calculating the cost. They are briefing their board.

If they come back with an offer — negotiate. The first offer will be low. That
is normal. Counter with the full package as leverage.

If they come back with nothing, or with an insulting offer — press GO. Execute
the blitz. No further warnings.

### 5.4. Mr C

Mr C should send his own Letter Before Action at the same time. Two letters
from two complainants, arriving together, with the same pattern evidence. This
is not a coincidence. This is coordinated. NatWest's legal team will see two
claims, one pattern, one press kit, and one private prosecution — and they will
calculate that this is cheaper to settle than to fight.

Mr C needs to answer his phone.

## 6. The Number

There is no single number. The number depends on:

1. **What the 999 recording shows.** If it shows fabrication — the number goes
   up dramatically. If it shows exaggeration — still strong. If it's basically
   accurate — the claim narrows to refusal of service and data protection.

2. **Whether Mr C participates.** Two claimants > one. Pattern > incident.

3. **Whether the FOI data shows a pattern.** If NatWest Brixton Road has been
   calling 999 on customers regularly, and people have been arrested and
   charged, the number is not about two claimants — it is about buying silence
   on a scandal.

4. **Whether NatWest settles before or after press.** Before press: cheaper.
   After press: much more expensive, and they can't un-ring the bell.

**Best realistic outcome (you + Mr C, full package, pre-press):**
£100,000–£200,000 combined.

**Best realistic outcome (post-press, pattern confirmed):**
£200,000–£500,000+ combined.

**Floor (you alone, no press, County Court only):**
£20,000–£40,000 net of the £4k overdraft.

## 7. Non-Financial Terms

Money is not the only thing to demand. Settlement should include:

| Term | Why |
| :--- | :--- |
| Written apology — from NatWest Group, not the branch | On record. Published. |
| Written confirmation that the 999 call was unjustified | Admission. Useful if anyone else comes forward. |
| Confirmation of what was reported to police | So you know what was said about you. |
| Deletion of all adverse data | Any flags, markers, security notes on your account/name. |
| Written confirmation to debt collectors | That the £4k is settled and no further collection action will be taken. |
| Staff retraining — de-escalation, threat assessment | Procedure change. Evidence that the bugs are patched. |
| Disclosure of pattern data | How many 999 calls in the last 10 years. Settle the enquiry question privately. |
| Right to publish the settlement terms | Or at minimum, no NDA. If they insist on an NDA, the price goes up. Silence has a cost. |
