## The Story in One Paragraph

A lifelong NatWest customer attended his home branch in Brixton to withdraw
cash. He showed his UK passport. The branch manager refused the withdrawal
without giving a reason. The customer expressed anger — verbally, calmly from
seven seconds in — called the manager an arsehole, and left. He walked home,
which is next door, and started making breakfast. While he was in his kitchen,
NatWest staff called 999. Metropolitan Police attended his address within
approximately five minutes. No crime had been committed. No threat had been
made. The customer had video of the entire incident. The manager had already
fled the branch floor before the customer said a word.

---

## The Hook

> NatWest called the police on a customer who had already gone home.

He wasn't on the premises. He wasn't armed. He wasn't threatening anyone. He
was making breakfast. The 999 call happened after the situation was over, in
respect of a man who was no longer there, about behaviour that was not a crime.

This is not a story about an angry customer. This is a story about a bank that
called 999 because a manager panicked — and the police showed up at a man's
home at 9 in the morning because of it.

## The Legal Questions

**1. Was the 999 call a false report?**

The complainant made no threat. Committed no crime. Was not on the premises
when police were called. If the 999 call described behaviour that the video
does not show — aggression, threats, violence — that is either wasting police
time (Criminal Law Act 1967, s.5(2)) or perverting the course of justice
(Common Law), depending on the degree of embellishment.

The 999 recording has been requested via Subject Access Request. When it
arrives, it will be compared word-for-word with the video. The delta is the
case.

**2. Was the refusal of service lawful?**

The complainant presented valid government-issued photographic identification
at his home branch. No reason was given for the refusal. This is a potential
breach of FCA Principle 6 (Treating Customers Fairly).

**3. Was the data disclosure to police lawful?**

The complainant's personal data — his identity, account details, and the
nature of the visit — was disclosed to Metropolitan Police via the 999 call.
No crime had been committed. No ongoing threat existed. Disclosure of personal
data to law enforcement requires a lawful basis. A manager's panic is not one.
This is a potential breach of UK GDPR / DPA 2018.

**4. Was the police response proportionate?**

Metropolitan Police dispatched officers to a residential address in Brixton at
priority response speed for a non-crime. Actual victims of actual violent crime
in Brixton do not routinely receive this response time. This is a potential
concern for the Independent Office for Police Conduct.

## The Bigger Question

This may not be a one-off.

A second individual — referred to here as Mr C — was arrested inside a bank
following a dispute over photo ID. His case is being investigated as part of a
pattern enquiry: does NatWest Brixton Road have a history of calling police on
customers? Have other people been arrested, charged, or even convicted on the
basis of similar calls?

Freedom of Information requests have been drafted to the Metropolitan Police
seeking data on how many 999 calls NatWest Brixton Road has made, how many
led to police attendance, and what the outcomes were.

If this branch has called 999 on customers before — and if people without
video evidence have been arrested or prosecuted — that is a different story
entirely.

## Background — The Complainant

Arthur Douglas Noel had held an account at NatWest Brixton Road since
childhood.

He attended the branch on 21 September 2021, two days after release from ten
days on remand. The charges were subsequently not pursued. His wallet had been
lost or stolen during arrest, leaving his passport as his only available ID.

He is pursuing this matter without a solicitor, using the private prosecution
right preserved by s.6(1) Prosecution of Offences Act 1985, alongside civil
County Court proceedings and regulatory complaints to the FCA, Financial
Ombudsman Service, ICO, and IOPC.

He has video. He has a transcript. He has filed Subject Access Requests. He
is waiting for the 999 recording.

   information to police via 999. When is that lawful? When is it not?
